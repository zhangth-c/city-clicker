# Designer's Note

Patchwork Borough now runs on a broader resource web instead of a single tax ladder.

## Core Intent

The economy is built around three overlapping loops:

1. `Settlement`
   Coins buy the first homes, homes create residents, and residents unlock the first supply districts.
2. `Support`
   Food, timber, and stone keep the borough physically alive. These are not prestige resources; they are the friction that stops every district from being a better cottage.
3. `Specialization`
   Goods, power, knowledge, appeal, and influence let districts diverge into distinct jobs instead of all becoming tax engines.

## Resource Roles

- `Coins`
  Liquid tax income and the universal baseline cost.
- `Residents`
  A gate, not a spendable pile. Population unlocks districts but does not solve every other problem for free.
- `Food`
  The first upkeep pressure. It forces the player to support growth instead of only buying homes.
- `Timber`
  Early structure and expansion material.
- `Stone`
  Durable construction and industrial scaling material.
- `Goods`
  Trade output that supports policy adoption and tourism infrastructure.
- `Power`
  Utility capacity for modern and engineered districts.
- `Knowledge`
  Mid-to-late game planning and technical capability.
- `Appeal`
  Soft prestige that unlocks cultural and tourism growth.
- `Influence`
  Civic leverage that turns a successful borough into a politically meaningful city.

## District Philosophy

Not every district should produce coins, and not every district should produce residents.

That rule matters because it creates actual borough roles:

- `Clockhouse Duplex` and `Red Cottage`
  Early settlement and tax base.
- `Hearth Grotto` and `Windmill Homestead`
  Raw support. These keep the town fed and stocked rather than rich.
- `Observatory Villa`
  The first knowledge district, signaling the shift from survival into design.
- `Pagoda Estate`, `Lighthouse Lodge`, `Gothic Keep`, `Treehouse Retreat`
  Cultural and civic identity. These produce appeal and influence more than population.
- `Brick Factory`, `Ice Dome Habitat`, `Glass Highrise`
  Infrastructure, utility, and high-density late-city throughput.

The result should feel less like "buy the highest number" and more like "stabilize the borough, then choose what kind of city it becomes."

## Policy Philosophy

Policies were rewritten to match the new economy:

- Early policies smooth the basics: clicks, roads, population, food, and raw build costs.
- Midgame policies reward committed branches: goods, knowledge, power, and festivals.
- Late policies reward composition and civic prestige rather than only raw production.

`Zoning Reform` is intentionally a composition reward, while `Civic Exchange` is the late conversion policy that turns influence into treasury strength.

## Prestige

Annexation still keys off peak residents and peak appeal because those represent the two things a borough must prove before it deserves to grow outward:

- it can sustain a large settlement
- it has become worth remembering

Prestige bonuses now lean toward replay smoothing instead of raw endgame inflation, helping the next borough stand up faster without removing the need to rebalance food, supplies, and specialization.
